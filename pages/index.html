Ichan Qala, located in the city of Khiva, Uzbekistan, is a stunning, fully intact inner town surrounded by towering mud-brick defensive walls up to 10 meters high. This historical complex served as a vital, final resting place for Silk Road caravans before they ventured across the desert towards Iran. Designated a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1990, it features over 50 historic monuments like the grand Kalta Minor Minaret and the Juma Mosque, alongside traditional dwellings that are still inhabited today. The unparalleled architectural harmony and urban composition make Ichan Qala one of the best-preserved examples of a medieval Central Asian city.
Registan Square in Samarkand, Uzbekistan, is recognized as Central Asia's most spectacular architectural site. It is framed by three magnificent, tiled madrasahs—Ulugh Beg, Sher-Dor, and Tilya-Kori—which once formed the commercial and cultural heart of the Timurid Empire. This UNESCO World Heritage site showcases the breathtaking artistry of the medieval Silk Road.
Ichan Qala is the remarkably preserved inner city of Khiva, Uzbekistan, entirely enclosed by imposing, 10-meter-high mud-brick walls. This UNESCO World Heritage site is an open-air museum housing dozens of historic monuments, including madrasahs and the famous Kalta Minor minaret. It remains a rare, living example of a medieval Central Asian city.
Shahi-Zinda, meaning "The Living King," is a sacred and stunning avenue of mausoleums located in Samarkand, Uzbekistan. This revered necropolis contains the grave of Qutham ibn Abbas, a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad, making it a major pilgrimage site. Built primarily during the 14th and 15th centuries, the complex is famous for its breathtaking, intricate display of glazed tilework in vibrant blue and turquoise hues, showcasing the peak artistry of Timurid architecture.
